About the Platform

PLEXOS Cloud is a Software-as-a-Service platform that enables energy market participants, utilities, system operators, planners, and analysts to model, simulate, optimize, and analyze power systems through a cloud-based environment. The platform provides scalable computation, collaboration capabilities, centralized data management, scenario analysis, and visualization tools to support energy market and transmission planning decisions.

UX Research

Problem Statement, Research Goals & Methodology

UX Research

Problem Statement

PLEXOS Cloud users — energy planners, transmission analysts, and market operators — faced a platform that had evolved from a legacy desktop application into a cloud environment without fundamental rethinking of its UX architecture. This created a systemic gap between the complexity of the domain and the usability of the tool.

5 Primary Challenges

1. Navigation Complexity
Users struggled to find core features across deeply nested menus, leading to task abandonment and reliance on documentation for basic workflows.
2. Steep Learning Curve
New users required weeks of onboarding before becoming productive. The interface exposed raw system complexity rather than guiding users through domain tasks.
3. Data Input Friction
Setting up models required navigating multiple disconnected screens and manually cross-referencing data — described as "tedious and error-prone" by multiple interviewees.
4. Lack of Collaboration
Teams working on shared models had no visibility into each other's edits, version history, or active sessions — forcing coordination via email and external tools.
5. Results Interpretation
Output visualizations were static and required export to Excel for any custom analysis, creating a broken workflow that added hours to every study cycle.

5 Current Pain Points

  • No search or filter across model objects — users scroll through hundreds of items manually
  • Model structure organized by software architecture, not user workflows or business problems
  • Users must visit 6+ separate locations to configure a single analysis scenario
  • 80–90% of displayed settings are irrelevant to the user's current study type
  • No copy/paste across objects or classes — every configuration must be repeated manually
Research Goals

Research Objectives

Primary Objective

Understand how energy market professionals use PLEXOS Cloud to complete real-world planning tasks — identifying where the UI creates friction, where mental models diverge from the system model, and what workflow patterns can inform a redesign that reduces cognitive load while supporting expert-level analysis.

Secondary Objectives

  • Identify the most common task flows and where users drop off or require workarounds
  • Map user mental models for data organization and model configuration
  • Determine which features are underused due to discoverability vs. genuine lack of need
  • Understand collaboration patterns across teams and how the tool currently supports or impedes them
  • Establish baseline usability metrics to measure redesign impact
Methodology

My Approach — 5-Method Research Methodology

I applied a mixed-methods approach combining behavioral observation, attitudinal research, and quantitative analytics. Each method was selected to address a specific research gap:

1
Stakeholder Interviews
Engaged product managers, engineering leads, and customer success to understand business constraints, existing knowledge of user pain points, and technical limitations on potential solutions.
2
Contextual User Interviews
Conducted 12 in-depth interviews with energy planners, market analysts, and system operators across ISOs, IPPs, and utilities. Sessions were 60–90 minutes and included task walkthroughs in live environments.
3
Pendo Analytics Review
Analyzed behavioral telemetry from Pendo to identify feature adoption rates, session drop-off patterns, and most-visited vs. least-visited UI areas. This data grounded qualitative findings in actual usage.
4
Card Sorting (Open + Closed)
Ran card sorting sessions with 18 participants to understand how users mentally categorize PLEXOS features and data — informing the redesigned information architecture and navigation structure.
5
Survey (n=50)
Deployed a structured survey to the broader user base covering feature importance, task frequency, satisfaction scores, and workflow preferences. Results were used to validate and prioritize interview findings at scale.
User Interviews

User Interviews

I conducted 12 structured contextual interviews with PLEXOS Cloud users from diverse organizational roles. Participants included Independent Power Producers (IPPs), Independent System Operators (ISOs), Utilities, and Load-Serving Entities (LSEs). Sessions were conducted over video with screen sharing to observe real workflows.

5 Key Questions

  1. Walk me through how you set up a typical model from start to finish. Where do you start, and what does it feel like?
  2. What parts of PLEXOS do you use every day? What do you avoid using, and why?
  3. When you get stuck or can't find something, what do you do — documentation, a colleague, or something else?
  4. If you could change one thing about PLEXOS to make your work easier, what would it be?
  5. How do you share work or collaborate with teammates using PLEXOS today?

Findings Summary

Users were highly proficient in the domain but consistently described the interface as a barrier, not a tool. The dominant theme was navigational overhead — the cognitive cost of knowing where to go, rather than knowing what to do. Power users had built elaborate personal workarounds (spreadsheet logs, step-by-step notes, bookmarked URLs) that indicated systemic usability failures in core workflows.
Research Findings

5 Key Insights

1
Mental model mismatch drives navigation failures
Users think in terms of study types and business problems ("run a gas dispatch study") but the UI is organized by technical objects ("classes → objects → properties"). This mismatch causes consistent disorientation.
2
Configuration is non-linear but the UI is linear
Energy planning inherently requires revisiting and adjusting parameters across interconnected model components. The current UI forces a linear sequence that doesn't map to how expert users actually work.
3
Collaboration happens outside the platform
All participants described using email, shared drives, or verbal handoffs to coordinate on models. None used any in-product collaboration feature — several were unaware these features existed.
4
Results are an afterthought in the current UX
After running a simulation, users consistently exported results to Excel for any meaningful analysis. The built-in results view was described as "a starting point, not a destination."
5
Onboarding relies entirely on social learning
New users learned PLEXOS almost exclusively by sitting with experienced colleagues or watching tutorials. In-product guidance offered no meaningful support for users building their first model.

Key Success Factors

What Would Make the Redesign Succeed

Through research synthesis and stakeholder alignment sessions, I identified five critical success factors that would determine whether the redesign truly served users:

Success Factor What It Means in Practice
Task-Oriented Navigation Information architecture must map to user workflows and study types, not the underlying data schema. Users should reach any core task in ≤3 clicks.
Progressive Disclosure Show only what users need for their current study type. Advanced options accessible but not visible by default — reducing cognitive load without limiting expert access.
Contextual Consistency Related configurations must live together. If two settings affect the same model component, they must appear in the same place regardless of how the system structures them.
Visible Collaboration Co-editing, version history, and change notifications must be prominently placed — not hidden in settings. Teams need ambient awareness of shared model state.
In-Platform Results Analysis The results experience must reach parity with what users currently do in Excel — custom comparisons, scenario overlays, and exportable charts — to end platform-switching.

Design Process

Whiteboarding, Current State Analysis & Usability Testing

Design Process

Whiteboarding Sessions

I facilitated three collaborative whiteboarding sessions with cross-functional stakeholders including product managers, engineering leads, domain experts, and customer success — using Miro as the collaborative workspace.

Session 1 — Problem Framing

Format: 2-hour session with 8 participants (product, engineering, CS)

Output: Shared problem map linking user pain points to system constraints; prioritized list of highest-impact UX failures

Key Decision: Focus the redesign on the model setup workflow first — the highest-frequency, highest-friction task across all user segments. Results and collaboration to follow in subsequent releases.

Session 2 — Information Architecture

Format: 3-hour working session with card sort outputs as input

Output: Proposed top-level navigation structure (6 categories), task-based sub-navigation design, and placement decisions for all major features

Key Decision: Adopt a top navigation bar (replacing the sidebar) to mirror spatial expectations users bring from other SaaS tools — supported by card sort data showing consistent mental model clustering.

Session 3 — Design Principles

Format: 90-minute session to align on design values before wireframing

Output: 8 agreed design principles serving as decision-making guardrails throughout the project

Key Decision: Every design decision evaluated against: "Does this support the user's domain task, or does it expose system architecture?" — preventing feature-team pressures from reintroducing technical complexity into the UI.

Process Flow

High-Level PLEXOS Usage Process — 6 Steps

Based on research synthesis, I mapped a 6-step high-level process representing the universal workflow for all user segments. This process map became the structural backbone for the redesigned information architecture.

1
Define Study
User selects study type (Energy Market, Transmission, Gas, etc.) and sets the horizon, temporal resolution, and geographic scope. This selection filters all subsequent configuration options.
2
Build / Load Data
User imports or builds the system model — generators, loads, transmission lines, fuel sources. Includes data validation and error checking before proceeding.
3
Configure Model
User configures simulation parameters, constraints, and settings specific to their study type. Context-aware filtering shows only relevant options.
4
Run Simulation
User submits the model for computation. Cloud-based execution provides real-time progress feedback, estimated completion time, and error notifications.
5
Analyze Results
User explores output data through in-platform visualizations, scenario comparison tools, and summary metrics. Annotation and scenario tagging support iterative analysis.
6
Share & Report
User exports results, shares models with collaborators, and publishes findings. Version history preserves the full study lineage for audit and reproducibility.

Design Opportunities, Principles & Key Screens

Solution

6 Design Opportunities

Research findings and the process flow analysis surfaced six high-impact design opportunities — each with a clear proposed solution and measurable expected impact:

Opportunity Proposed Solution Expected Impact
Task-centric navigation Replace sidebar with top navigation organized by the 6-step workflow. Each tab corresponds to a phase, not a data type. Reduce navigation clicks per task by 60%
Study-type filtering Study type selection at model creation persists across all screens and filters properties to show only relevant settings — hiding the other 80–90% by default. Reduce irrelevant settings shown by 80%
Contextual configuration panel Side panel showing all properties for a selected object in a single scrollable view — replacing the current multi-page navigation pattern. Eliminate need to visit 6+ locations for single task
In-platform results Interactive results dashboard with scenario overlay, custom chart builder, and annotated comparison — closing the Excel export loop for 80% of analysis use cases. Reduce external tool dependency for results analysis
Visible collaboration Persistent collaboration panel showing active users, recent edits, and version history. Real-time change indicators on affected model components. Shift coordination from email to in-platform
Progressive onboarding In-product checklist for first-time users, contextual tooltips on first encounter with domain terms, and guided model setup flow for common study types. Reduce time-to-first-simulation for new users by 50%
Design Principles

8 Design Principles for PLEXOS Cloud UX

These principles were established collaboratively during the third whiteboarding session and served as decision-making guardrails throughout design and development.

1. Domain-first, system-second
Every UI decision must serve the user's energy domain task. System architecture is never a valid reason to expose complexity to users.
2. Progressive disclosure
Show only what users need now. Advanced options are accessible, not invisible — but they require deliberate expansion, not passive scanning.
3. Contextual cohesion
Everything related to a task belongs together. Splitting related settings across multiple pages to match the data model is not acceptable.
4. Expert efficiency
Power users must be able to move fast. Keyboard shortcuts, bulk actions, search, and filters are first-class citizens — not afterthoughts.
5. Visible state
Users must always know: where they are, what state the model is in, and what just changed. No silent actions — every significant operation provides feedback.
6. Collaborative by default
Sharing, version history, and co-editing are core features presented at the same hierarchy as modeling and simulation — not buried in settings.
7. Error recovery, not prevention alone
Validate inputs early, but assume users will make mistakes. Every destructive action must be reversible. Version history is the safety net for iterative work.
8. Consistent pattern language
The same UI pattern must always mean the same thing. Users who learn one section of the platform should transfer that knowledge to every other section without re-learning.
Design

Home Page & Dashboard

The redesigned home page serves as a personalized command center — giving users immediate access to recent work, team activity, and key status indicators without requiring navigation to find their starting point.

Purpose

Reduce the time from login to productive task to under 30 seconds by surfacing the most relevant context (recent models, active simulations, team changes) directly on the landing screen.

5 Key Components

  • Recent Models Panel: Last 5 accessed models with study type, last modified date, and active user indicators
  • Active Simulations Widget: Real-time progress for running jobs with estimated completion time and error alerts
  • Team Activity Feed: Chronological log of collaborator changes across shared models — with model name, change type, and timestamp
  • Quick Start: Prominent "New Model" button with study type picker — reducing model creation to a single entry point
  • Notifications Center: Simulation completions, collaboration invites, and system alerts consolidated in one dismissible panel

Design

End-to-End Model Setup

The model setup workflow was redesigned as an 8-step guided flow — replacing the current approach where users must discover each step independently across disconnected screens. The flow adapts dynamically based on the selected study type, hiding irrelevant steps and configuration options.

1
Name & Describe Model
User provides model name, description, and optional tags. Templates available for common study configurations.
2
Select Study Type
Study type selection (Energy Market, Transmission, Gas, etc.) triggers dynamic filtering of all subsequent steps. "What is this?" tooltip provides contextual guidance.
3
Set Horizon & Resolution
Define simulation time range and temporal granularity with sensible defaults pre-populated based on study type. Visual calendar picker for date ranges.
4
Import / Build System Data
Upload CSV/XML or build the system from scratch using the object library. Inline validation flags missing required fields before proceeding.
5
Configure Properties
Contextual side panel presents all properties for each selected object type. Only study-relevant properties shown by default — "Show All" toggle for expert access.
6
Set Simulation Parameters
Algorithm settings, solver tolerances, and performance options — filtered to study-relevant parameters. Presets available for common configurations.
7
Validate & Review
Model health check runs automatically, flagging errors, warnings, and recommendations. Users can resolve issues inline before submission.
8
Submit & Monitor
One-click submission to cloud compute. Progress tracker shows phase completion, estimated time, and alerts. Users can continue other work while simulation runs.

Legacy Application Screens & Redesign

The following screens illustrate the contrast between the legacy PLEXOS interface and the redesign direction. Three core screens were selected as the primary scope for the initial redesign release.

Current State — 3 Core Legacy Screens

  • Screen 8.4 — Object Tree: Dense sidebar tree showing all model objects in a flat, unsortable list. No search. No grouping by function. Objects organized by class name (software architecture), not user task.
  • Screen 8.5 — Properties Grid: Tabular property display with no filtering, no contextual grouping, and all properties shown simultaneously regardless of relevance. Expert users scroll through 200+ rows to find the 5 they need.
  • Screen 8.6 — Results Viewer: Static output table with no visualization. "Export to CSV" is the primary interaction — users immediately leave the platform for Excel analysis.

Redesign Proposals

  • 8.6 — Redesigned Object Editor: Study-type-aware panel. Objects grouped by function (Generation, Transmission, Load). Search bar persists at top. Recently accessed objects pinned.
  • 8.7 — Contextual Properties Panel: Slide-out side panel with property groups collapsed by default. Only study-relevant properties shown. "Show Advanced" toggle for power users. Inline save with undo.
  • 8.8 — Interactive Results Dashboard: Chart-first results view with scenario switcher, date range slider, and metric selector. Export available but no longer the default path.

Advanced Features — 8.9

Advanced capabilities planned for Phase 2 include: Global Model Search (search across all objects, properties, and values in a model), Cross-Object Comparison (view a single property across all objects in a class simultaneously), and Bulk Property Edit (select multiple objects and update shared properties in one action).

Challenges

6 Challenges & How We Resolved Them

1. Stakeholder Resistance to Navigation Overhaul
Root Cause: Engineering and some PMs believed the sidebar was familiar to expert users and that changing it would cause short-term regression.
Challenge
Push-back from internal teams who feared disrupting expert workflows and triggering a wave of support requests post-launch.
Solution
Ran a comparative usability test with both sidebar and top-nav prototypes. Top-nav outperformed on every metric, including expert user tasks. Data ended the debate.
2. Domain Complexity vs. Design Simplicity
Root Cause: Energy market modeling is genuinely complex — some of that complexity must be exposed to expert users.
Challenge
Risk of over-simplifying and removing access to advanced configurations that power users relied on, even if infrequently.
Solution
Progressive disclosure framework: defaults for 80% of use cases, "Show Advanced" toggle always available. No features removed — only repositioned.
3. Technical Constraints on Real-Time Collaboration
Root Cause: The existing data architecture was not designed for concurrent editing — significant engineering rework required.
Challenge
Full real-time co-editing was not feasible in the initial release window due to backend architecture limitations.
Solution
Phased approach: Phase 1 released visible presence (who's online) and read-only activity feed. Real-time co-editing scoped to Phase 2 with dedicated engineering sprint.
4. Inconsistent Mental Models Across User Segments
Root Cause: ISOs, IPPs, utilities, and LSEs use PLEXOS for fundamentally different study types with different conceptual frameworks.
Challenge
Card sort results showed divergent categorization patterns across segments — no single navigation structure scored above 70% for all groups individually.
Solution
Adopted the 6-category structure that scored highest across all segments combined (78% agreement in closed sort validation) with study-type filtering for segment-specific personalization.
5. Pendo Data Gaps
Root Cause: Pendo instrumentation was incomplete — key user flows lacked event tracking, making behavioral data unreliable for some sections.
Challenge
Could not rely on Pendo data alone for the properties configuration flow — the area with the highest self-reported friction had the least behavioral telemetry.
Solution
Supplemented with session recordings and targeted usability tests. Simultaneously worked with engineering to add missing Pendo events to establish a baseline for post-launch measurement.
6. Scope Creep Risk During Design
Root Cause: As research surfaced pain points across the entire platform, the project scope risked expanding to cover every issue discovered.
Challenge
Stakeholders wanted all problems solved in a single release — a scope that would have extended the timeline by 12+ months and diluted design quality.
Solution
Created a phased roadmap with clear Phase 1 (navigation + model setup), Phase 2 (results + collaboration), and Phase 3 (advanced features) — with measurable success criteria for each phase.
Lessons Learned

8 Lessons

1
Domain expertise is table stakes for complex enterprise UX. Without genuine fluency in energy market concepts — study types, simulation hierarchies, domain terminology — it would have been impossible to understand what users meant when they described their pain. I invested heavily in domain learning before any design work.
2
Behavioral data and self-reported data tell different stories — you need both. Pendo showed that the collaboration feature had low usage. Interviews revealed users didn't know it existed. Neither data source alone would have surfaced the right solution (discoverability, not functionality).
3
Whiteboarding sessions accelerate alignment faster than any document. The three whiteboarding sessions with stakeholders produced decisions that weeks of async review couldn't reach. Shared visual thinking eliminates the translation layer between research and action.
4
Test with data, not opinions. When the navigation debate threatened to stall the project, a direct usability comparison ended it in two weeks. Building the test — not writing a longer brief — was the right move.
5
Progressive disclosure is a philosophy, not just a pattern. Deciding what "simple by default" means for expert users required continuous calibration. The right level of disclosure is different for a new user vs. a 10-year veteran — the design must serve both without insulting either.
6
Phased roadmaps protect design quality. Shipping the navigation and model setup flow first — before collaboration and results — allowed us to get real-world usage data before designing the next phase. Sequential release beats parallel bloat.
7
The metric you can't measure today, instrument today. Pendo gaps at the start of the project meant we had no baseline for the properties configuration flow. Adding instrumentation early — even before the redesign shipped — would have given us a true before/after comparison.
8
User quotes are the most persuasive stakeholder communication tool. Metric improvements are compelling. Hearing "I haven't opened Excel once this week" in a user's own words changes a room. Always pair quantitative results with qualitative voice.
Key Takeaway

Key Takeaway

Complex enterprise tools don't fail because their users aren't expert enough — they fail because the interface doesn't respect what expertise actually looks like in practice. PLEXOS users knew exactly what they needed to do; the platform just made every step harder than it needed to be. The role of design in this project was not to simplify the domain — it was to remove the friction between a user's intent and the system's capability. When we got out of the way, users were faster, more confident, and more collaborative than they had ever been in the product's history.
